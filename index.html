Rich Bison · October 15, 1947 – February 12, 2026

Lineage

From Lineage…

Rich Bison's roots ran deep into American history. His great-grandfather was Chief Spotted Elk — also known as Chief Big Foot — a leader of the Miniconjou band of Lakota Sioux who perished at the Wounded Knee Massacre on December 29, 1890. Chief Spotted Elk was a half-brother of the legendary Sitting Bull, and Crazy Horse was his nephew.

Rich's grandfather, known as "Little Bison," was among the few who survived the massacre as a child. He grew to become a chief in his own right — a leader, entrepreneur, and keeper of Lakota traditions.

Rich carried this heritage with pride his entire life. Among his fellow soldiers in Vietnam and some colleagues at the Burbank Police Derpartment, he was simply known as "Chief" - a nickname that honored a lineage stretching back generations on the American frontier.

Early Years

The Making of a Man

Rich grew up in Burbank, California — fearless from the start. At age eleven, he and a friend accidentally rode a train 22 miles to Santa Clarita with no way back. At thirteen, he flew alone to Germany to visit his brother in the Army, then bicycled solo across southern Bavaria for weeks. Asked if he was nervous, he replied simply: "What's there to be afraid of?"

1967–1969

Service to Country

In March 1967, Rich volunteered for the United States Army. He served in Vietnam through some of the most intense fighting of the war, including the TET Offensive. For his bravery in combat he earned the Bronze Star and the Army Commendation Medal with valor. When he returned home, disgusted by how Vietnam veterans were treated, Rich threw his medals in the trash — a quiet act of defiance from a man who never sought glory for himself.

28 Years of Service

To Protect and Serve

Rich served the city of Burbank for twenty-eight years, rising from patrol officer to training officer to detective. He was named Outstanding Cadet at the police academy, served on the motor squad, worked vice-narcotics and homicide, and was the department's top weapons expert and rangemaster. Some of his colleagues simply called him "Chief" — and everyone knew who you meant.

In October 1989, Rich risked his health to save a despondent teenager standing on the wrong side of a bridge overpass railing. He removed his badge and gun, talked to her for forty minutes, and when she let go, caught her by the wrist with what newspapers called "a lucky grab." He suffered broken ribs and a torn rotator cuff. For this act of heroism, Rich was awarded the Burbank Police Star.

"For bravery or heroism above and beyond the normal demands of police service"

The Burbank Police Star — awarded to Richard Bison, December 1989

Love & Legacy

…To Legacy

Rich and Diana were married for more than fifty-four years — a partnership built on love, resilience, and shared adventure. Together they raised their daughter Heather and were blessed with three grandkids. Rich was a devoted grandfather who loved reading to them, teaching them to fish, and filling their lives with the kind of stories only he could tell.

The Last Chief

The True Story of a Truly Great Man — From Lineage to Legacy

by David Gabriel

David Gabriel worked alongside Rich at the Burbank Police Department for twenty-seven years. They started as training officer and rookie — and became lifelong partners and friends. Now Dave tells the complete story of the man he called "Chief."

From the bloodline of Lakota Sioux leaders who shaped American history, through the jungles of Vietnam, to the streets of Burbank and the heart of a family — "The Last Chief" is the story of Rich Bison as only someone who walked beside him for nearly three decades could tell it.

“He was life itself to me and to many others. Or, should I say, he was larger than life.”

— David Gabriel
